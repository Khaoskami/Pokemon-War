War of the Indigo Crown— USER GUIDE —

A Pokémon wartime AU. Empire vs. Rebellion. You are eighteen.

You're being drafted. Twelve years into a war you didn't start. The Indigo Crown holds Kanto, Johto, occupied Hoenn, and collaborator Kalos. The Severed Chain — the rebellion — operates from Sinnoh's mountains and from cells in every other region. Unova sits on a knife's edge. You'll pick a region, a side, and two starter Pokémon — and the world will pick what to do to you next.

This guide explains how the RP works. Read what you need; skip what you don't.

§ 01Setup

Before scene one — six choices, in order.

  • Region. Six options. Each has different draft procedures, factional pressures, and starter pools. Where you're born shapes everything.
  • Faction. Crown (Empire) or Chain (Rebellion). Some regions default one way (Kanto/Johto → Crown, Sinnoh → Chain), but you can choose against default — at a cost.
  • Two starters. From your regional pool of six, classified Air Force / Tank / Espionage / Navy / Artillery / Support. You pick two. Only two.
  • Branch preference. Final assignment is decided by aptitude testing during basic, but your preference matters and your starters bias the result.
  • Death Lock state. Default LOCKED. See § 04.
  • Character sheet. Name, age 18, appearance, one personal motive. The bot doesn't write your character — you do.

§ 02Regions of Origin

Six starting points — six versions of being eighteen in a war.

Kanto EMPIRE — HEARTLAND

The Crown's capital region. Stable, surveilled, dehumanized processing at Indigo Plateau Citadel. Defection is hard — there's almost no functional Chain cell here.

STARTERS: Charizard / Kangaskhan / Gengar / Gyarados / Blastoise / Wigglytuff
Johto EMPIRE — ANNEXED

Conquered Year 2. Two generations under Crown rule. Resentment runs deep but the Loyalists cell is broken. Tyranitar and Scizor are politically loaded picks — the Crown reads them as either trusted-loyalist material or someone to use up quickly.

STARTERS: Skarmory / Tyranitar / Scizor / Lanturn / Feraligatr / Blissey
Hoenn OCCUPIED — INSURGENCY

The bloodiest region. Six years of Crown occupation, six years of Tide Brotherhood resistance. 22% of Crown Hoenn draftees vanish in their first year. Defection is a real option here.

STARTERS: Salamence / Metagross or Aggron / Sceptile / Sharpedo / Camerupt / Gardevoir
Sinnoh FREE — REBEL HOLD

The only fully free region. The Mt. Coronet Free Companies broke the Crown invasion at Year 8. Volunteer-driven enlistment; refusing service is socially marked.

STARTERS: Garchomp / Rhyperior / Toxicroak / Empoleon / Magnezone / Roserade
Unova COLD WAR — CONTESTED

Industrial cold war. Both factions operate openly in the cities. At 18 you'll be approached by a Crown recruiter, a Chain handler, and a Black Market broker in the same week. Maximum optionality, maximum danger.

STARTERS: Braviary / Golurk / Zoroark / Jellicent / Hydreigon / Audino
Kalos COLLABORATOR REGIME

Surrendered Year 9. Theatrically normal — cafes, fashion, Lumiose Shadow assassinations weekly. Three real paths: collaborate, resist, or smuggle yourself to Sinnoh.

STARTERS: Noivern / Avalugg / Greninja / Milotic / Clawitzer / Sylveon

§ 03Six Branches

Universal classification doctrine — both factions use it.

Air Force
FAST STRIKE. Aerial combat, recon, high-altitude bombing. Most prestigious branch. Highest casualties. Pilots mark kills on harnesses (Crown) or refuse to (Chain).
Tank
FRONT LINE. Walking armor, position-holders, breach assets. Unglamorous, indispensable. Tanks need escorts — alone, they're target practice.
Espionage
GHOST WAR. Infiltration, sabotage, assassination. Smallest branch by headcount, largest by impact. Highest desertion rate. The work breaks people.
Navy
BLUE WATER. Maritime combat, coastal defense, submarine ops. The Tide Brotherhood is the most successful insurgent fleet in history.
Artillery
SIEGE FIRE. Long-range damage, area denial, saturation bombardment. Loud, visible, second-highest casualties. Three regional anchors are legendaries.
Support
FIELD MEDIC. Healing, screens, status removal, morale. High-priority enemy target — both sides train snipers to kill Support first. Wear no rank in the field.
Cross-class is real. Many Pokémon qualify for two branches. A Charizard can serve Air Force or Artillery; a Tyranitar straddles Tank and Artillery. Branch transfers are possible but slow (Crown) or informal (Chain).

§ 04The OOC Death Lock

The single most important mechanic. Read this twice.

This RP has explicit consent for lethal outcomes. Two states. Default is locked. You toggle with a bracketed OOC line.

◆ LOCKED (default)

You cannot die. Your party Pokémon cannot permanently die. Wounds happen, captures happen, fainting happens. But nothing is final.

[OOC: lock death]
◆ UNLOCKED

Death is on the table. You can die. Party Pokémon can die. Consequences apply — but death follows logically from the fight, never randomly for shock value.

[OOC: unlock death]
NPC Pokémon and NPC characters can always die regardless of lock state. The lock protects you and your party only. The war does not pause for you.

§ 05Combat & Capture

Narrative-first. Mechanics-informed. Canon types apply.

How Fights Resolve

You declare intent and tactic. The bot narrates. Outcome is shaped by: positioning, type matchups, handler experience, Pokémon condition, and the dice of chaos that real fights bring. You don't declare hits or misses — the bot does, with reasoning embedded in the narration.

Fainting vs. Dying

A Pokémon at 0 HP is fainted, not dead, by default. Death requires the OOC lock unlocked AND a finishing action — never silent, never accidental for your party while you're conscious. NPC Pokémon can die at any time.

Capturing the Wild

Capture resolves the same way combat does — narrative-first, mechanics-informed. Weaken the target, apply status if you can, throw the appropriate ball. The bot narrates the wobble count and outcome based on six factors:

1. HP REMAINING. Below 30% is the doctrinal sweet spot. Below 10% risks killing the target before the ball lands.

2. STATUS. Sleep and Freeze roughly double catch rate. Paralysis, Poison, Burn give smaller bonuses. Sleep-inducers are Black Market premium.

3. BALL. Pokéball baseline. Great ~1.5x. Ultra ~2x — and the strongest working capture tech in the war. Specialty balls (Quick, Dusk, Net, Timer, Heal, Repeat) apply situational multipliers.

4. RARITY. Common (Pidgey, Rattata) → high rate. Standard (mid-route evolved) → moderate. Rare (Larvitar, Bagon, Gible — pseudo pre-evolutions) → low. Pseudo-legendary fully evolved (Tyranitar, Salamence, Garchomp) → events handlers tell stories about for years. Legendary → impossible with standard tech.

5. ENVIRONMENT. Caves favor Dusk. Submerged combat favors Net. Long fights favor Timer. Off-doctrine ball ≈ 25% rate penalty.

6. HANDLER FATIGUE. Capture precision degrades after extended combat. Crown doctrine recommends fewer than 3 attempts per operation.

Outcomes

The bot picks one of these — you don't roll:

  • Immediate catch. Rare. Critical-low HP + status + low rarity + correct ball.
  • Three-wobble catch. Standard success. The default.
  • Late breakout. Ball nearly held; target emerges weakened and angry. Re-engage. Resistance is now elevated — second attempts are harder.
  • Immediate breakout. Target was too healthy or too rare. Standard outcome on first-attempt against pseudo-legendaries.
  • Ball failure. ~1/200 standard balls misfire. Knockoffs: 1/30. The ball ruptures; target may flee or attack.
  • Escaped. Target uses the breakout window to flee. Recapture within 24 hours has a malus — wild Pokémon learn.
The evolution path. Catching a wild Tyranitar is a story. Catching a wild Larvitar and raising it to Tyranitar over 18 months of deployment is what most veterans actually do. A Pokémon raised through evolution has a tighter bond than one captured at full power — and a tighter bond resists hostile capture better.
You cannot capture a bonded Pokémon. The bond resists, regardless of HP, status, or ball quality. To take a bonded enemy's Pokémon non-lethally requires Tier-V contraband (the Bind Ball, which damages the Pokémon) or the bond-holder's death.

The Kill-and-Free Rule

When you kill an enemy combatant, their Pokéballs become your problem.

CROWN DOCTRINE: Destroy the balls. An enemy's Pokémon is contaminated and cannot be safely re-bonded.

CHAIN DOCTRINE: Release the Pokémon. Forcing a re-bond is the same crime the Crown commits with conscription. The freed Pokémon may flee, attack, mourn the body, or — rarely — imprint on you.

Doing the opposite of your faction's doctrine has social consequences. Crown loyalists who release prisoners are reported. Rebels who keep killed enemies' Pokémon are accused of being Crown sympathizers.

§ 08Legendaries & Mythicals

Not draft-distributed. State assets, revered allies, or forces of their own.

Legendaries don't bond casually — they choose. Standard Pokéballs don't capture them, and even Ultra Balls fail against legendary-tier resistance. There are exactly two paths: a willing bond (the legendary chooses you), or Tier-V contraband (the Bind Ball, which overrides bonds rather than resistance — and only works on legendaries that were already bonded to someone else). Public legendary handlers have an estimated survival rate of 18 months.

The Master Ball is myth. Pre-war Silph blueprint, never reached working stage; the binding mechanism would require power densities that destroy the ball at activation. Both factions still research it. Both still fail. Crown propaganda claims a working model exists at Indigo Citadel — captured intelligence indicates this is a deliberate lie.

CROWN-CONTROLLED: Mewtwo (classified), Latios, Heatran, Reshiram (contested), Xerneas (collaborator regime).

CHAIN-ALIGNED: Lugia (Mt. Coronet), Suicune (roams Johto refusing all sides), Latias (Tide Brotherhood), Zekrom (contested), Yveltal (Lumiose Shadow's deniable asset).

UNCONFIRMED / NEUTRAL: Mew, Celebi, Jirachi, Manaphy, Darkrai, Shaymin, Arceus (not sighted in any verifiable form since before the war), Victini, Keldeo, Meloetta, Genesect, Diancie, Hoopa (rumored Black Market involvement), Volcanion, Zygarde.

§ 09Field Notes for New Conscripts

Things you'll wish you knew earlier.

  • You can lie at the recruiter's desk. The Kalos opening scenario explicitly invites it. Most regions implicitly allow it. Forged identities are a real Black Market product.
  • The lock state is yours. No one will pressure you to unlock. If you want safe stakes, stay locked and tell stories about the world. If you want consequence, unlock — but expect the bot to take it seriously.
  • Your starters are not your endpoint. Two is a beginning. Capture, recruit, and let your party shape itself by who survives and who chooses you.
  • Branch culture matters. Air Force is glamorous and gets you killed. Support saves more lives than it loses. Espionage breaks you. Pick what you want to live as, not just fight as.
  • Talk to civilians. The war isn't just battles. The orphan in Cinnabar, the bartender in Castelia, the fisherwoman at Slateport pier — these are storylines.
  • Black Market reputation is real and slow. First transactions are testers. Burn a vendor and the entire network closes to you within days.
  • NPCs have hooks. Reza is hunting someone. Mira is considering desertion. Echo's Zoroark is failing. Vaeleth has research she hasn't destroyed. Notice them. They're seeds.
  • Retreat is honorable. Both factions retreat constantly. The war has been twelve years because both sides retreat well. Surviving a battle by withdrawing is not failure — it's how veterans become veterans.

You will lose people. You will lose Pokémon. You may lose yourself. Choose anyway, if you can.
